**Summary**
This paper demonstrates that real quantum theory (using only real numbers instead of complex numbers) cannot be experimentally distinguished from standard complex quantum theory in any finite network experiment. The authors prove that previous claims about being able to falsify real quantum theory relied on untestable mathematical assumptions rather than observable differences.

**Key Takeaways**

  - The distinction between product-state independence (mathematical constraint) and operational independence (observable correlations) is crucial for experimental falsifiability
  - Every finite network correlation achievable in complex quantum theory is also achievable in real quantum theory with the same locality structure
  - The equivalence extends to arbitrary finite sequential multipartite protocols involving channels and measurements
  - Both theories support different mathematical pictures while producing identical observable correlations

**Why it matters**
This resolves a fundamental question about whether complex numbers are truly necessary in quantum mechanics, showing that the mathematical structure may be less constrained by experiment than previously thought.
